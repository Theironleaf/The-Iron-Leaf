THE LONG
GAME.

Not anti-ageing. Not biohacking. The deliberate, evidence-based building of capability that lasts — measured in decades.

The Iron Leaf Position

We do not teach anything we would not teach to a 60-year-old who wants to remain capable, independent, and strong for the rest of their life. Every recommendation passes this filter.

Longevity culture has split into two unhelpful extremes — expensive biohacking protocols promising marginal gains at significant cost, and fatalistic resignation that ageing simply happens to you. Neither reflects the evidence.

The research is remarkably consistent: a small number of high-leverage interventions — cardiorespiratory fitness, resistance training, metabolic health, sleep, and social connection — account for the overwhelming majority of modifiable longevity outcomes. Most of the rest is marginal.

This section covers the four pillars of the Deep Roots Protocol — the framework governing how we think about building a long, capable, independent life.

Topic 01

VO2 MAX &
CARDIORESPIRATORY FITNESS.

The single strongest modifiable predictor of all-cause mortality currently known to medical science — stronger than any single drug, supplement, or lifestyle factor studied to date.

★★★★★Strong Evidence — Large-scale cohort studies

VO2 max measures the maximum rate at which your body can consume oxygen during intense exercise — a proxy for the combined efficiency of your heart, lungs, blood, and muscle mitochondria working together. A landmark 2018 study of over 120,000 individuals found cardiorespiratory fitness to be a stronger mortality predictor than smoking cessation, hypertension management, or diabetes control.

Unlike many longevity markers, VO2 max is highly trainable at any age. Both high-intensity interval training and steady-state aerobic work improve it — with evidence suggesting a combination of both produces superior results to either alone.

The Myth

"Cardio is bad for longevity — strength training is all that matters."

The Evidence

Both are essential and address different physiological systems. Strength training builds and preserves muscle mass; cardiorespiratory fitness is the single strongest mortality predictor known. Neither replaces the other in a complete longevity strategy.

The Iron Leaf Recommendation

Combine 2–3 weekly sessions of moderate steady-state cardio (zone 2, conversational pace) with 1 weekly high-intensity interval session. This combination produces the strongest evidence-supported cardiorespiratory adaptations available.

Topic 02

MUSCLE MASS
PRESERVATION.

Sarcopenia — age-related muscle loss — begins earlier and progresses faster than most people realise. Resistance training is the single strongest known countermeasure.

Without resistance training, adults typically lose 3–8% of muscle mass per decade after age 30, with the rate accelerating after 60. This loss directly predicts frailty, fall risk, and loss of independence in older age — making muscle mass one of the most consequential longevity variables.

The encouraging finding: resistance training remains highly effective at any age, including in individuals beginning in their 70s and 80s. Muscle tissue retains its capacity to adapt and grow throughout life — the limiting factor is consistent stimulus, not biological inevitability.

Common Questions

FREQUENTLY
ASKED.

What people ask about longevity, healthspan, and ageing well.

No. Resistance training studies consistently show meaningful strength and muscle mass gains in individuals beginning in their 70s and 80s. Cardiorespiratory fitness similarly remains trainable at any age. The biological capacity for adaptation persists throughout life — the limiting factor is starting and remaining consistent, not your starting age.

The evidence base for most popular longevity supplements (NAD+ precursors, rapamycin, various peptides) remains preliminary at best, with most human trials small or absent. The interventions with the strongest evidence — exercise, sleep, nutrition, social connection — are free or low-cost. We recommend mastering these before considering expensive, unproven supplementation.

Laboratory testing provides the most accurate measurement but is not necessary for most people. Many fitness wearables provide reasonable estimates. More practically, simply tracking improvement in submaximal heart rate at a given pace, or completing standardised tests like a 1.5-mile time trial periodically, gives you useful trend data without specialised equipment.

If forced to choose one: cardiorespiratory fitness, given its outsized predictive power for all-cause mortality across the research literature. But the honest answer is that no single intervention substitutes for the combination of cardiorespiratory fitness, resistance training, sleep, and nutrition working together — they address different physiological systems and the evidence supports addressing all four.
