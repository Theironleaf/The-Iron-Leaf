Topic 02

HYPER
TROPHY.

The science of muscle growth — distilled from decades of research into the variables that actually matter, and the ones the industry exaggerates.

★★★★★Strong Evidence — Multiple meta-analyses

Muscle hypertrophy is driven by three primary mechanisms: mechanical tension, metabolic stress, and (to a lesser degree) muscle damage. Of these, mechanical tension — achieved through progressive overload across a sufficient range of motion — is the dominant driver according to the current research consensus.

Training volume, measured in weekly sets per muscle group, shows a clear dose-response relationship up to a point. Schoenfeld's meta-analyses establish 10–20 weekly sets per muscle group as the landmark zone for most trained individuals — below this, growth is suboptimal; above it, returns diminish and recovery capacity is challenged.

The Myth

"You need to train each muscle group to complete failure every set to maximise growth."

The Evidence

Training within 1–3 reps of failure produces equivalent hypertrophy to training to absolute failure, with significantly less fatigue and faster recovery — allowing higher sustainable training volume over time.

Training frequency — how often you train each muscle group weekly — matters less than total weekly volume, provided that volume is achievable in fewer, larger sessions or distributed across more frequent smaller sessions. Both approaches produce similar outcomes when volume is equated.

The Iron Leaf Recommendation

Train each major muscle group 2–3 times weekly with 10–20 total weekly sets, mostly within 1–3 reps of failure. Progress load or reps over time. Prioritise compound movements. This single paragraph contains more practical value than most paid programmes.

Topic 03

PROGRAMMING &
PERIODISATION.

A training year is not a single block. It is a sequence of phases, each with a distinct purpose, building toward sustained long-term progress without burnout or plateau.

Periodisation is the deliberate variation of training variables over time — volume, intensity, exercise selection — to manage fatigue, drive adaptation, and prevent the plateaus that occur when the same stimulus is repeated indefinitely.

The simplest and most evidence-supported model for most lifters is linear or undulating periodisation — gradually shifting from higher volume, lower intensity phases toward lower volume, higher intensity phases across a training block, then deloading and repeating.

PhaseDurationIntensityPurpose
Accumulation4–6 weeks65–75% 1RMBuild volume tolerance, muscle growth
Intensification3–4 weeks75–85% 1RMConvert volume to strength expression
Peaking1–2 weeks85–95% 1RMExpress maximal strength, low fatigue
Deload1 week40–60% 1RMDissipate fatigue, consolidate adaptation
A 12-Week Strength Block
Weeks 1–4
ACCUMULATION PHASE

Higher volume, moderate intensity. Build work capacity and muscle through 12–18 weekly sets per muscle group at 65–75% intensity.

Weeks 5–8
INTENSIFICATION PHASE

Volume drops, intensity rises. Fewer sets, heavier loads. Strength expression begins to manifest from the accumulated muscle base.

Weeks 9–11
PEAKING PHASE

Low volume, high intensity. Practising near-maximal lifts with full recovery between sessions. Performance testing in week 11.

Week 12
DELOAD & RESET

Dramatically reduced volume and intensity. Allow full recovery before beginning the next training block with a higher baseline.

Topic 04

OLYMPIC
LIFTING.

The most technically demanding and athletically valuable lifts in strength training. Total body power, coordination, and explosive force production in a single coordinated movement.

01
SET-UP & FIRST PULL

Bar over midfoot, hips above knees, shoulders over bar. The first pull lifts the bar from floor to knee with controlled, gradually increasing velocity.

02
TRANSITION & SECOND PULL

The power position — bar at mid-thigh, knees re-bend slightly. Then explosive triple extension of hip, knee, and ankle generates maximum bar speed.

03
TURNOVER & CATCH

The lifter pulls themselves under the bar as it reaches peak height, catching it in a front rack position with stable trunk and full mobility.

04
RECOVERY & STABILISATION

Standing up from the catch position with control, demonstrating full-body stability under load before the jerk or lowering sequence begins.

A Word of Caution

Olympic lifts are technically demanding and carry injury risk without proper coaching. We strongly recommend learning these movements under qualified supervision before training them independently — particularly the catch positions, which require significant shoulder and hip mobility.

Topic 05

MOVEMENT
QUALITY.

Strength built on poor movement patterns is strength borrowed against your future joints. Quality first. Load second.

Movement quality refers to the ability to control your body through a full, appropriate range of motion with stability and coordination — independent of how much weight is involved. It is the foundation that strength training builds upon, not a separate consideration.

Functional movement screening identifies asymmetries and limitations before they become injuries. Compound, multi-joint exercises that demand coordination — squats, deadlifts, presses, pulls — are simultaneously the best strength builders and the best movement quality tests available.

The questions we hear most often about strength training, programming, and progression.

That said, total weekly volume matters more than frequency itself — training a muscle group once weekly with sufficient volume can still produce comparable results to higher frequency approaches, provided recovery and intensity are well managed.

Neural adaptations — improved coordination and motor unit recruitment — produce noticeable strength gains within 2–4 weeks for beginners. Measurable muscle growth typically takes 6–8 weeks to become visible, though it begins at the cellular level much earlier. The Iron Leaf approach is built around years, not weeks — but early progress is real and motivating.

Heavier loads are more efficient for building maximal strength specifically, but for pure muscle growth, moderate loads with sufficient effort work just as well.

Progress load or volume gradually — no more than 5–10% increases week to week. Prioritise movement quality and full range of motion before adding load. Include a structured deload every 4–6 weeks. Address mobility limitations proactively rather than reactively. Most training injuries result from rapid, poorly managed progression rather than the exercises themselves.
